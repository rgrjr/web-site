The table below shows sunset, twilight, and moon rise/set times for the Boston area for Tuesday climbing nights in 2004. The familiar phases of the moon (new, first quarter, full, last quarter) are actually just instants when the sun-earth-moon angle passes through multiples of 90 degrees [1]. The intervals between these instants are labeled "waxing" or "waning" depending on whether the moon appears to be growing or shrinking, and "crescent" or "gibbous" depending on whether the phase is closer to new or full. The phase interval between the first quarter and the full moon is therefore called the "waxing gibbous" phase; this is the phase that is most likely to be of use when taking down toproping setups in the early evening, as the moon is already up before the sun sets, and bright enough to make a difference. The best such nights are highlighted in bold; on the other nights, the moon rises too late to be helpful. (The moon on 24 August may not be bright enough.)

After climbing, we usually start taking the ropes down at sunset, but some diehards may keep climbing nearly until the end of twilight, especially if an almost-full moon is high enough in the sky, or the climbing location has a clear view to the west, or the sunset comes too early. Or sometimes just because we're diehards.
